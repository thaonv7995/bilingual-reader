Normally, Eastern Time (ET) is 5 hours behind UTC but on 29th Apr 2024, Daylight Savings Time (DST) was in effect in ET (which means, the clocks had already been shifted 1 hour forward in New York) and so, ET was only 4 hours behind UTC on this date. India does not observe DST and so, Indian Standard Time (IST) is always 5.30 hours ahead of UTC. You may verify these numbers by printing nyTime.getOffset() and indiaTime.getOffset(). They will print -04:00 and +05:30 respectively. That is why the 10AM in ET was translated to 7.30PM in IST instead of 8.30PM on this day. This example shows that ZonedDateTime handles the adjustments required due to DST automatically.

Note that I have used ET and IST only for illustrating the logic. You do not need to remember the offsets for any time zone or the dates and the amount of shift required due to DST for any location.

Translating into gaps and overlaps created due to DST

The translation from ET to IST was easy, but what if, after the translation, the date and time at the target location doesn’t exist? This could happen when the target location observes DST and the translated date/time happens to fall at the time when the clocks have been moved forward.

For example, given that ET is 5 hours behind UTC and the DST starts on 10th March 2024 at 2AM (this means, as soon as the clocks in New York hit 2AM on this day, they are moved forward by 1 hour to show 3AM), what if you try to convert 10th March 2024 7.30AM UTC to ET?

Normally, the time in ET would be 2.30AM but on 10th March 2024, 2.30AM doesn’t exist in ET!
