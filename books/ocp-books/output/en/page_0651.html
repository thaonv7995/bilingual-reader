The above code prints 2024-03-10T03:30-04:00[America/New_York], which shows that Java automatically moves the time forward to 3.30AM and the zone offset to -4 due to DST when the time at the destination falls into the gap.

The reverse happens when DST ends. The clocks are shifted back by 1 hour. So, for example, since DST ends at 2AM on 3rd Nov 2024 in New York, as soon the clocks hit this date/time, they are moved back to 1AM. Thus, people in New York get to live the hour between 1AM to 2AM twice on that day! During the first hour the Zone offset is -4 (because DST was in place at that time) and during the second hour, it is -5. This sounds cool but what happens when a time from another time zone that doesn't observe DST during this period is translated to New York time?

Let's work it out with the same two time zones - UTC and ET. Imagine that you are translating the time continuously from 4AM UTC onwards to ET. Translating any time before 5AM UTC to ET is easy. Since DST is "ON" in ET during that period, the time in ET would just be UTC minus 4 (instead of UTC minus 5). So, 5AM UTC would be 1AM ET, no doubt.
