January 1, 1970 00:00:00.000 at Greenwich. (Greenwich is the name of the town in UK where the Royal Observatory is located. Time at this location was chosen for historical reasons). Time at this location is called Greenwich Mean Time (GMT). The same time has been adopted globally as the reference time and is also called Coordinated Universal Time or UTC for short. You can think of time as a series of milliseconds plotted on the x-axis, where 0 is called January 1, 1970 00:00:00.000 GMT. Every millisecond elapsed since the epoch moves you forward on this axis.

Humans, on the other hand, see time in terms of hours, minutes, and seconds of a specific day, month, and year, at a particular location. For example, to computers, the point 1735711200000 on the time line means 1735711200000 number of milliseconds have elapsed since the epoch. But to humans, this point is actually 6AM 1st January 2025 at Greenwich. The same point in time could also be interpreted as 1AM on January 1st 2025 in New York or 10PM on Dec 31st 2024 in Los Angeles. Old Java classes mix these two very different ideas in the same class. This is evident from the fact that java.util.Date actually just stores the number of milliseconds that have elapsed since epoch and has no idea about what date it is. Yet, the name of the class is Date!

The new Date/Time API addresses this concern well and makes it a lot easier to perform routine operations such as date arithmetic and printing.
